The Life of Srinivasa Ramanujan

A mathematical genius from India who saw patterns in numbers that nobody else could see.

December 22, 1887
Born in Kumbakonam, a town in Tamil Nadu, southern India. His family was poor, but his mother believed her son would become a great scholar.
1897–1904
Attended school and fell in love with mathematics. He taught himself advanced math from library books. Teachers noticed he was solving problems nobody had asked him to solve.
1904
Passed his secondary exams but refused to study other subjects. He got a scholarship to college but lost it because he only wanted to study math. He was brilliant at one thing and refused to do anything else.
1909–1912
Worked as a clerk earning barely any money, while filling notebook after notebook with thousands of equations. He had no formal training and never went to university, but he invented mathematics that took decades to understand.
1913
Sent letters to the famous British mathematician Godfrey Hardy showing his formulas. Hardy was stunned. He invited Ramanujan to Cambridge University in England.
1914–1919
At Cambridge, Ramanujan published papers that changed mathematics forever. He invented new formulas for pi, discovered patterns in prime numbers, and created a branch of math called partition theory. But he was lonely, far from home, and fell very ill.
1920
Returned to India, hoping the warm climate would heal him. He died on April 26 at age 32. He filled four notebooks with thousands of formulas, some still being studied today.

Magic Squares — Ramanujan's Puzzle

Ramanujan loved these! Fill in the numbers 1–9 so that every row, column, and diagonal adds to 15. Try it yourself!

1
What is a magic square? A 3×3 grid of numbers where every row, column, and diagonal adds to the same number (called the "magic constant").
2
For numbers 1–9: The magic constant is always 15. (Hint: the center is usually 5!)
3
Ramanujan invented magic squares with special properties. Some of his squares had dates hidden in them!

Taxicab Numbers — The 1729 Story

The most famous number in mathematical history! It's both the sum of two cubes in TWO different ways.

1
The Legend: Hardy visited Ramanujan in the hospital and said, "I came in taxi number 1729. That's a dull number." Ramanujan replied, "No! It's very interesting!"
2
Why? Because 1729 = 1³ + 12³ = 9³ + 10³. It's the smallest number that's the sum of two cubes in TWO different ways!
3
Ramanujan immediately knew this without checking! He could see deep patterns in numbers that other mathematicians took hours to find.
1729 = 1³ + 12³ = 9³ + 10³
1 + 1728 = 729 + 1000 = 1729

Try to find other taxicab numbers:

15625: 5³ + 25³ = 25³ (this one has 3 ways!)

Fun Facts About Ramanujan

📚
Self-Taught Genius
He never attended university and never had a math teacher. He learned everything by reading old textbooks and inventing new math.
🧮
4 Notebooks
He filled 4 notebooks with over 3,800 formulas. Mathematicians are still discovering what they mean and how to prove them!
💭
Dreams and Intuition
He claimed his family goddess would whisper formulas to him in dreams. He saw the answers first, then figured out the proof later.
🌍
Poor to Brilliant
He went from being a poor clerk earning barely enough to eat to becoming a Fellow of the Royal Society in 1918 — one of England's highest honors!
✍️
Hardy's Discovery
G.H. Hardy said that Ramanujan's letter was the most astonishing mathematical letter he'd ever received. It changed his life and India's reputation in science.
🔬
Only 32 Years Old
He died in 1920 at just 32 years old. If he'd lived longer, he might have won a Nobel Prize in mathematics (if one existed for math!).
🎥
"The Man Who Knew Infinity"
His life story was made into a movie in 2015 starring Dev Patel and Jeremy Irons. It won awards for best cinematography.
π
Pi Formulas Forever
His formulas for calculating pi were SO efficient that they're still used today to break world records for computing digits of pi.
🏆
Partition Function
He discovered patterns in "partitions" (ways to write numbers as sums). His formula works perfectly, but he never proved why!
🇮🇳
India's Pride
December 22 (his birthday) is celebrated as "National Mathematics Day" in India. His face appears on Indian postage stamps!
🚀
Vegetarian and Strict
He was a strict vegetarian which made it hard to eat in England. He struggled with the cold weather, loneliness, and unfamiliar food.
⚡
Instant Calculation
He could calculate square roots and cube roots in his head faster than anyone could write them down. His brain was wired differently!
