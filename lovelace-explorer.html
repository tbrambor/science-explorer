Ada Lovelace Explorer

Ada Lovelace (1815-1852)

The World's First Computer Programmer

1815

Born in London

Ada Augusta Byron was born to one of the world's most famous poets, Lord Byron. However, her father left when she was just one month old.

1815-1833

An Unusual Childhood

Her mother, worried that Ada might inherit her father's "poetic madness," insisted on math and science education instead of poetry. Ada was taught languages, mathematics, sciences, and music—an unusual education for a girl in the 1800s.

1833, Age 17

Meeting Charles Babbage

Ada attended a party where she met Charles Babbage and saw his incredible invention: the Difference Engine. This machine could perform mathematical calculations automatically. Ada became fascinated with his work.

1833-1843

Becoming an Analyst

Ada became Babbage's close friend and collaborator. She studied his designs for an even more powerful machine: the Analytical Engine—a machine that could be programmed to perform different operations. She called herself "an Analyst (& Metaphysician)."

1843

A Historic Publication

Ada translated an article about the Analytical Engine from French to English. But she didn't stop there! She added her own notes—which were 3 times longer than the original article. Her notes included diagrams, explanations, and something revolutionary...

1843 (Note G)

The First Algorithm

In "Note G," Ada wrote out a step-by-step set of instructions (an algorithm) for the Analytical Engine to compute Bernoulli numbers. This was the first computer program ever written! She understood that machines could follow complex instructions, not just do simple math.

1843

A Visionary Prediction

Ada predicted something no one else could imagine: computers could do FAR more than just numbers. She wrote that machines could compose music, create graphics, and manipulate other kinds of information. Over 100 years before computers existed, she saw the future!

1843-1852

Illness & Legacy

Ada struggled with serious illnesses throughout her life. She died in 1852 at only 36 years old. Her brilliant work was nearly forgotten for 100 years until people rediscovered her contributions.

1980s & Today

Recognition

Ada's legacy was finally celebrated! The U.S. Department of Defense named a programming language "Ada" in her honor in 1980. Today, she's recognized as the world's first computer programmer, and October 9th is Ada Lovelace Day, celebrating women in STEM.

Understanding Binary

Computers think in a language called binary using only two digits: 0 (off) and 1 (on). Every letter, number, color, and sound on your computer is made up of combinations of 0s and 1s!

Each position in a binary number represents a power of 2: the rightmost position is 1, then 2, 4, 8, 16, 32, 128, and 256. When a position has a 1, we "turn it on" and add its value. When it has a 0, we "turn it off" and add nothing.

What is an Algorithm?

An algorithm is a set of step-by-step instructions for solving a problem. It's like a recipe for a cake—you follow steps in order to get a result. Ada Lovelace wrote the first algorithm ever to be executed by a machine!
