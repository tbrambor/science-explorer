The Life of Leonhard Euler

From child prodigy to the greatest mathematician the world has ever seen.

1707
Born in Basel, Switzerland. His dad was a pastor who also loved math.
1720 (age 13)
Enrolled at the University of Basel — younger than most college students today! Studied under the famous Johann Bernoulli.
1727 (age 20)
Moved to St. Petersburg, Russia to join the Academy of Sciences. Started publishing papers at an incredible pace.
1735 (age 28)
Solved the Seven Bridges of Königsberg problem — accidentally inventing an entirely new field of math called graph theory.
1738 (age 31)
Lost sight in his right eye — possibly from staring at the sun during astronomy work. Did it slow him down? Not even a little.
1741 (age 34)
Moved to Berlin at the invitation of Frederick the Great. Spent 25 years there, writing hundreds of papers.
1766 (age 59)
Returned to St. Petersburg. Lost sight in his other eye — now completely blind. His response? He increased his output, dictating papers to assistants.
1783 (age 76)
Passed away in St. Petersburg. Left behind 800+ papers and 100+ books. It took nearly 50 years after his death to publish everything he'd written!

Euler's Identity — "The Most Beautiful Equation"

Let's build up to it step by step. First, meet the 5 most important numbers in math.

eiπ + 1 = 0

This single equation connects 5 fundamental numbers. Let's learn what each one means!

0
Zero — Nothing
The starting point. The number that means "nothing is there." It took humans thousands of years to even invent it!
1
One — The Building Block
Every whole number is built by adding 1s together. It's the foundation of counting. 1, 1+1=2, 1+1+1=3...
π
Pi — The Circle Number
Take ANY circle. Divide its circumference by its diameter. You always get π ≈ 3.14159... It goes on forever and never repeats!
e
Euler's Number — Nature's Growth Rate
e ≈ 2.71828... It shows up wherever things grow naturally — populations, money earning interest, even radioactive decay. It's the "speed of growth."
i
The Imaginary Number
What number times itself equals -1? None that you know! So mathematicians invented one and called it i. Sounds crazy, but it turns out to be incredibly useful — it makes the equation below possible.

The Magic: e + i = Circles?!

Here's the wild part. Euler discovered that when you combine e and i, you get... circular motion.

1
Imagine a number line, but in 2D — the horizontal axis is regular numbers, and the vertical axis is imaginary numbers (multiples of i).
2
Euler proved that eiθ (where θ is an angle) traces a perfect circle on this 2D plane! The horizontal position is cos(θ) and the vertical position is sin(θ).
3
When you go halfway around the circle (θ = π = 180°), you land at exactly -1 on the number line.
4
So eiπ = -1. Add 1 to both sides and you get: eiπ + 1 = 0. That's it. That's the whole identity!

The Seven Bridges of Königsberg

The puzzle that invented graph theory.

In 1735, the city of Königsberg (now Kaliningrad, Russia) had 7 bridges connecting 4 land masses separated by the Pregel River. The townspeople asked: "Can you walk through the city crossing each bridge exactly once?" Euler proved it's impossible — and in doing so, invented a whole new branch of math.

Euler's Insight: He turned the map into a graph — dots (nodes) connected by lines (edges). He realized you can only walk every edge exactly once if at most 2 nodes have an odd number of connections.

Count the bridges at each landmass:
• Island = 5 bridges (odd!)
• North bank = 3 bridges (odd!)
• South bank = 3 bridges (odd!)
• East bank = 3 bridges (odd!)

All 4 are odd! You'd need at most 2 odd nodes for it to work. Since all 4 are odd, it's mathematically impossible — no matter how clever you are.

This simple idea launched graph theory, now used everywhere from GPS navigation to social networks to recommendation algorithms.

Mind-Blowing Euler Facts

This guy was unreal.

📝
800+ Papers
Euler published more math papers than any human in history. If you read one per day, it would take over 2 years to get through them all. For nearly 50 years after his death, journals were still publishing his backlog of unpublished work.
🧠
Mental Calculator
After going completely blind, Euler could do enormous calculations entirely in his head. He once settled an argument between two students by mentally computing a sum to 50 decimal places. His brain was basically a supercomputer.
📖
Memorized the Entire Aeneid
Euler could recite Virgil's Aeneid — a 10,000-line Latin poem — from memory, and could tell you the first and last line of every single page in his copy of the book. His memory was photographic.
⚡
Solved the Basel Problem
At age 28, he solved a puzzle that had stumped the world's best mathematicians for 90 years: the exact sum of 1/1² + 1/2² + 1/3² + … = π²/6. He became instantly famous. What do squares have to do with circles?!
🔤
He Named Everything
The symbols π, e, i, Σ (sigma for sums), and f(x) for functions? Euler either invented or popularized them all. Every time you write a math symbol, you're probably speaking Euler's language.
🔥
Blindness Made Him Faster
When Euler went blind in his second eye at age 59, he didn't slow down — he sped up. Without the distraction of sight, he produced roughly one paper every week for the rest of his life. His best work came after he couldn't see.
🎓
Master's Degree at Age 16
Euler earned his Master's degree from the University of Basel when he was just 16 years old. His thesis compared the philosophies of Descartes and Newton. Most kids that age are learning to drive!
👶
13 Children, No Problem
Euler had 13 kids and reportedly did some of his best math while holding a baby in one arm and a chalk in the other. He said his children were one of his greatest joys.
💬
"He calculated as easily as men breathe"
That's an actual quote about Euler from the famous mathematician Arago. Other mathematicians said trying to keep up with Euler was like trying to outrun a horse.
🌍
Touched Every Branch of Math
Calculus, number theory, graph theory, topology, optics, astronomy, mechanics, fluid dynamics, music theory — there is almost no area of math or physics that Euler didn't contribute to. He basically built the foundations of modern mathematics.
🏠
Survived a Burning House
In 1771, already blind, Euler's house caught fire. A servant carried him out on his back through the flames. Euler lost his home and many possessions — but went right back to doing math.
💀
Died Doing Math
On September 18, 1783, Euler was calculating the orbit of the newly-discovered planet Uranus, had dinner, drank tea, played with his grandchild — and then passed away. Math was literally the last thing on his mind.
