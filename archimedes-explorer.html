The Life of Archimedes

Mathematician, physicist, engineer, inventor, and ancient legend.

c. 287 BC
Born in Syracuse, Sicily — one of the most powerful Greek cities. His father, Phidias, was an astronomer.
c. 270 BC (teens)
Traveled to Alexandria, Egypt to study at the great Library of Alexandria — the world's most famous center of learning. Studied under followers of Euclid.
c. 260 BC
Returned to Syracuse and began working for King Hiero II. Invented the Archimedes Screw — a device for lifting water that is still used today, over 2,200 years later!
c. 250 BC
Wrote On the Measurement of the Circle, where he trapped π between polygons and proved 3 + 10/71 < π < 3 + 1/7. The most accurate estimate for centuries.
c. 250 BC
Solved King Hiero's crown puzzle — discovered how to test if the crown was pure gold using water displacement. Supposedly ran through the streets shouting "Eureka!"
c. 240 BC
Proved the law of the lever mathematically and famously declared: "Give me a place to stand and I will move the Earth."
214–212 BC
The Roman siege of Syracuse. Archimedes designed terrifying war machines — giant claws that lifted ships out of the water, catapults, and (legend says) mirrors that set ships on fire with focused sunlight.
212 BC
Syracuse fell. A Roman soldier found Archimedes drawing circles in the sand. Archimedes said "Do not disturb my circles." The soldier killed him. He was about 75 years old.

The Pi Machine — Squeezing a Circle

Archimedes' brilliant method for calculating π without a calculator.

1
The problem: π is the ratio of a circle's circumference to its diameter. But circles are curved — how do you measure a curve precisely?
2
Archimedes' trick: Draw a polygon inside the circle and another outside it. The circle's circumference is trapped between the two polygon perimeters.
3
More sides = better estimate. A triangle is rough. A hexagon is better. A 96-sided polygon? That's what Archimedes calculated by hand — getting π accurate to about 3.1416!

Eureka! — The Buoyancy Lab

King Hiero suspected his goldsmith cheated him. Archimedes figured out how to prove it — in the bath.

1
King Hiero gave a goldsmith pure gold to make a crown. But he suspected the smith kept some gold and mixed in cheaper silver. How to test it without melting the crown?
2
While taking a bath, Archimedes noticed the water level rose when he got in. The bigger the object, the more water it pushes aside (displaces).
3
He realized: gold is denser than silver, so a gold crown would displace less water than a mixed one of the same weight. He could compare the crown to a lump of pure gold!
4
He was so excited he leaped out of the bath and ran through the streets of Syracuse shouting "Eureka!" (Greek for "I have found it!") — reportedly without any clothes on.

The Law of the Lever

"Give me a place to stand and I will move the Earth." — Archimedes

1
A lever is a bar that pivots on a fixed point called the fulcrum (the triangle in the middle).
2
Archimedes proved that a lever balances when: Weight₁ × Distance₁ = Weight₂ × Distance₂.
3
This means a small weight far from the fulcrum can balance a heavy weight close to it. That's how you can lift something much heavier than you!

Mind-Blowing Archimedes Facts

2,200 years later, he's still one of the greatest.

🔥
The Death Ray
Legend says Archimedes used giant mirrors to focus sunlight onto Roman ships, setting them on fire from the shore. Modern experiments have shown this is theoretically possible — MIT students partially replicated it in 2005!
🦀
The Claw of Archimedes
He built a giant crane-like claw mounted on the city wall. It would reach down, grab Roman ships by the bow, lift them up, and flip them over. Roman soldiers called it "the iron hand." They were terrified.
🧮
Counted Grains of Sand in the Universe
In his paper The Sand Reckoner, Archimedes invented a new number system just to calculate how many grains of sand would fill the entire universe. His answer: about 8 × 10⁶³. He basically invented scientific notation 2,000 years early.
⭕
"Do Not Disturb My Circles"
When a Roman soldier found Archimedes during the fall of Syracuse, he was drawing geometric circles in the sand and refused to stop. His alleged last words were "Do not disturb my circles." The Roman general Marcellus was reportedly furious at the soldier for killing him.
🌊
The Archimedes Screw
He invented a screw-shaped device inside a tube that lifts water when you turn it. It was used to irrigate fields and drain ship bilges. Incredibly, the exact same design is still used today in some wastewater treatment plants and developing countries.
🏃
Naked Street Run
After his bathtub discovery about displacement, Archimedes was so excited he reportedly ran through the streets of Syracuse completely naked, shouting "Eureka! Eureka!" It's history's most famous scientific moment — and most embarrassing.
🪐
Built a Mechanical Planetarium
Archimedes built a mechanical device that showed the motions of the Sun, Moon, and planets. When the Roman general Marcellus captured Syracuse, this was the one item he kept for himself. It may have been similar to the famous Antikythera mechanism.
📐
2,000 Years Ahead of His Time
In a lost work called The Method, Archimedes used techniques incredibly close to modern calculus — nearly 2,000 years before Newton and Leibniz. The manuscript was only rediscovered in 1906, hidden under a prayer book.
💎
He Outsmarted a Cheating Goldsmith
By comparing how much water a crown displaced versus the same weight of pure gold, he proved the goldsmith had mixed in cheaper silver. The goldsmith was caught and punished — all thanks to bath time.
🏛️
Tombstone Wish
Archimedes was so proud of proving that a sphere inside a cylinder takes up exactly 2/3 of the cylinder's volume that he asked for it to be carved on his tombstone. The Roman orator Cicero found the tomb 137 years later and confirmed the engraving was there.
⚔️
Held Off Rome for 2 Years
The Roman Empire was the most powerful military force on Earth, yet Archimedes' war machines held off their siege of Syracuse for nearly 2 full years. One man's engineering genius vs. an entire empire — and he almost won.
🧠
Newton and Gauss Called Him the GOAT
Isaac Newton and Carl Friedrich Gauss — two of the greatest mathematicians ever — both considered Archimedes to be the greatest mathematician of all time. Even geniuses had a hero, and it was this guy from Syracuse.
